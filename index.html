Business Structure — What’s At Risk vs What’s Protected

Most people start a business without understanding what is actually at risk. The way your business is structured determines your level of exposure, protection, and long-term opportunity.

Sole Proprietor — No Structure

As a sole proprietor, you and your business are legally the same.

Using Your SSN

Your business operates under your personal Social Security Number (SSN). There is no separation between you and your business activity.

  • Your personal bank account can be affected
  • Your car can be affected
  • Your home may be at risk depending on the situation
  • Your income is directly tied to business activity
  • Business debt can follow you personally

There is no legal separation between you and your business.

LLC — Limited Liability Company

An LLC creates separation between you and your business—but only if it is operated correctly.

Get Your EIN

After forming your LLC, you must obtain an EIN (Employer Identification Number) from the IRS by going to IRS.gov and applying directly.

  • Replaces your SSN for business use
  • Required to open a business bank account
  • Used for business credit
  • Keeps your personal identity separate

What Can Still Go Wrong

  • Mixing personal and business money
  • Personally guaranteeing loans
  • Poor recordkeeping
  • Not operating like a real business

If handled incorrectly, the protection can break down.

Trust + Holding Structure

This structure separates ownership, control, and operations into layers.

How It Works

  • Operating LLC: runs the business
  • Holding Company: owns the business
  • Trust: owns the holding company

Final Structure:

Trust → Holding Company → Operating Business

What This Does

  • Separates ownership from operations
  • Creates structure for multiple businesses
  • Organizes assets more strategically

Generational Wealth Protection

This setup helps organize ownership beyond one person and supports long-term control.

  • Keeps ownership structured across generations
  • Supports long-term asset organization
  • Creates a system for succession planning
  • Separates ownership from daily operations

This structure does not eliminate risk or taxes automatically.

Business Expenses & Tax Positioning

When your business is structured and operated correctly, certain expenses used for business purposes may be treated as business expenses.

  • Business-use portion of a vehicle (car, truck, trailer)
  • Equipment and tools
  • Office-related expenses
  • Marketing and branding
  • Website and digital tools

Proper structure allows you to track and manage expenses which may reduce taxable business income.

Expenses must be ordinary, necessary, and used for business purposes. Personal expenses do not automatically qualify.

The level of structure you choose determines how exposed or protected your business and personal life may be.
